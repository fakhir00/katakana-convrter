What Are Gairaigo (Foreign Loanwords)?

Gairaigo (外来語, literally "words from abroad") are foreign words that have been adopted into the Japanese language. The vast majority come from English, though Portuguese, Dutch, French, and German have also contributed significantly throughout history.

These words are almost always written in Katakana, which serves as a visual marker that immediately tells Japanese readers: "this word has foreign origins." Understanding common Katakana words is essential because they appear everywhere — on menus, product labels, signs, websites, and in everyday conversation.

💡
English Speaker Advantage

As an English speaker, you already know the meaning of hundreds of Katakana words! Once you can read the Katakana script, you'll discover that many Japanese words are simply English words adapted to Japanese pronunciation rules.

Watch Out: False Friends (Wasei-Eigo)

Some Katakana words look like English but have different meanings in Japanese! For example: マンション (mansion) means "apartment/condo" not a large house. バイキング (viking) means "all-you-can-eat buffet." ナイーブ (naive) means "sensitive" in Japanese.

Часто Задаваемые Вопросы

What are the most common Katakana words?

The most common Katakana words include コーヒー (coffee), パソコン (computer), テレビ (TV), レストラン (restaurant), ホテル (hotel), バス (bus), and タクシー (taxi). These appear in everyday Japanese life.

Why are foreign words written in Katakana?

Katakana serves as a visual marker for words of foreign origin (gairaigo). This tells Japanese readers immediately that the word comes from another language, helping distinguish it from native Japanese words written in Hiragana or Kanji.

How many Katakana words are used in daily Japanese?

Approximately 10-15% of words in modern Japanese are Katakana loanwords. In tech-heavy, fashion, or food contexts, this percentage can be much higher — sometimes up to 30-40% of vocabulary on a menu or tech article is Katakana.

What are Wasei-Eigo (Japanese-English) words?

Wasei-Eigo are Katakana words that appear to be English but either don't exist in English or have different meanings. Examples include マンション (apartment, not mansion), フロント (reception desk), and サラリーマン (office worker).
