What Is English Name to Katakana Conversion? 🎌

When you move to Japan, fill out Japanese forms, or create content for Japanese audiences, your English name needs to be written in Katakana—the Japanese script reserved for foreign words, names, and loanwords. Converting an English name to Katakana isn't a simple letter-by-letter translation; it's a phonetic conversion based on how your name sounds to Japanese speakers. For a comprehensive overview of the Katakana writing system, see Wikipedia's guide to Katakana.

Why English Names Need Katakana in Japan 🇯🇵

Japanese uses three writing systems simultaneously, each with a distinct purpose:

ScriptPurposeUsed For
HiraganaNative Japanese wordsGrammar particles, native words
KanjiMeaning-based wordsNouns, verbs, adjectives
KatakanaForeign words & namesEnglish names, loanwords, emphasis

Your English name belongs in Katakana because it's a foreign word in Japanese. Whether you're:

  • Filling out Japanese immigration forms
  • Signing up for Japanese services
  • Creating a business card for Japan
  • Introducing yourself in Japanese

Your name must be written in Katakana for Japanese readers to pronounce it correctly.

The Challenge: English Sounds Don't Always Map to Japanese 🎯

Japanese has only 46 basic Katakana characters representing about 71 distinct sounds. English has approximately 44 phonemes—and many don't exist in Japanese. This creates conversion challenges:

Converting English names to Katakana follows a systematic phonetic process:

  1. Pronunciation Analysis — The converter identifies how the name sounds, not how it's spelled
  2. Phoneme Mapping — Each English sound maps to the closest Japanese sound

  4. Katakana Assignment — Each syllable gets its corresponding Katakana character

Handling Difficult English Sounds 🎤

#### The L and R Problem 🔊

Japanese doesn't distinguish between L and R sounds—both map to the same Japanese consonant family (ラリルレロ / ra-ri-ru-re-ro). This means:

English NameKatakanaJapanese Pronunciation
LisaリサRisa
RobertロバートRobāto
ClarkクラークKurāku
LindaリンダRinda

Tip: If your name contains L sounds, Japanese speakers will pronounce it with an R-like sound. This is normal and expected in Japanese.

#### The V and B Challenge 👄

Japanese doesn't have a true V sound. Historically, V sounds were written as B sounds. Modern Japanese sometimes uses combinations like ヴ (vu), but B remains more common:

English NameTraditionalModernCommon Usage
Davidデビッドデヴィッドデビッド (most common)
Kevinケビンケヴィンケビン (most common)
Steveスティーブスティーヴスティーブ (most common)

#### Th Sounds → S or Z 👅

English "th" sounds don't exist in Japanese. They're approximated:

English SoundJapanese EquivalentExample
Voiceless "th" (think)S (ス/セ/ソ)Smith → スミス (Sumisu)
Voiced "th" (this)Z (ズ/ゼ/ゾ)Thomas → トマス (Thomasu)

Each syllable is analyzed for its phonetic components:
